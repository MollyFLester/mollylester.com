HUBBLE

For a DESIGNATION exploratory project, we were asked to redesign the online learning experience using Design Thinking. Considering the recent explosion of online learning, we were asked to consider what was keeping it from being as effective as in-person

Role

UX Researcher
Interaction Designer

Deliverables

Personas / Task Flows /
Wireframes / Prototypes

Client

Designation Labs

EMPATHIZE & DEFINE

QUANTITATIVE DATA

We constructed an online survey In order to gain a foundational understanding of our users and the key players in the industry. While garnering survey responses, we conducted a competitive analysis and subsequent SWOT analysis to discover opportunities for innovation and solutions in the market.

Key Takeaways

1.

Users in the 20-30 age range put more trust into online learning

2.

Most users of a free online learning service had one course that they never fully completed

3.

Stated they would be willing to pay for a learning service if it was a “legitimate” service

4.

University affiliation was a big factor in deciding to trust a service

5.

The overwhelming source for help on many competitor’s sites were forums

QUALITATIVE DATA

Using the agile methodology, my team began by creating a research plan to define how we would attack the problem. Together we collected extensive quantitative data regarding how and why people use online learning tools, as well as common pain points.

Key Takeaways

1.

Confused about their progress within the course

2.

Felt alone in the learning process due to a lack of social interaction and helplessness when encountering obstacles

3.

Bored and didn’t feel like an active participant while learning

4.

Felt that it was hard to find answers through forum posts

5.

Desired the flexibility to work at their own pace

6.

Wanted a more detailed overview of the entire unit before starting

SYNTHESIZE

AFFINITY DIAGRAMMING

Organizing our raw data and insights allowed us to identify patterns in our research and focus our scope to online coding schools. With our newly defined scope, we were able to create a problem statement, design principles, and personas.

Key Takeaways

1.

Consumers’ perception of credibility is often based on the price of the service or an affiliation with an accredited university

2.

Motivation is derived from what a consumer could do with their new knowledge after completing the learning experience

3.

Interactivity during the lessons played a huge role in whether the user felt like they retained information

4.

The number one draw of online learning was its promotion of flexible learning styles

5.

Classmate interaction is a secondary motivation found with in-person learning that is not being replicated in the online setting

6.

Current market is missing out on the opportunity for a more personalized learning experience

PROBLEM STATEMENT

Online learning platforms require increased human connection to foster an interactive environment for users. This interactive learning experience will generate and sustain interest, improve information retention, and offer users a stronger sense of personalization.

DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Empower the User

Create an atmosphere where the student feels as if they are creating the answers, and empower them to choose how they learn.

Progress is Everything

Show progression and provide the user with positive feedback upon task completion.

Everything Should Lead to Real Life

The end result must be applicable to the user’s continuing careers.

Don't Overwhelm the User

Ensure information is presented in digestible portions that cumulatively build into a larger skill.

Humanity is Key

Although the online learning process is performed independently, the user should never feel alone in their journey.

Be Forgiving

Reassure the user that mistakes are a part of the learning process and help steer them toward the correct path.

Delightful at Every Step

Every interaction should contribute to a joyful overarching experience.

Dig In, Not Out

Supplemental information and relevant tools should be easily accessible, but organized in a way that does not interfere with the learning process.

PERSONAS

My team saw that the primary distinguishment between types of learners was their starting point: are they new to the topic or trying to further an existing career? Each type of learner uses social interaction for a different reason, necessitating a solution with the flexibility of both autonomy and collaboration. Offering this choice empowers the user and allows them to personalize the learning experience.

TECHWEEK

Techweek, a festival featuring “all things tech,” recently expanded and wanted to refresh its design strategy. The festival had previously commissioned UX work to revamp its website calendar and wanted a full website redesign to match.

Role

UI Designer

Deliverables

Style Tiles / Style Guide
Final Mockups / Responsive Mockups

Client

Techweek

EMPATHIZE & DEFINE

STAKEHOLDER GOALS

In our beginning meeting with Stakeholders at Techweek, we solidfied their primary goals for the website redesign. These not only involved a fully fleshed out UI design, but a restructuring of their current website.

Key Takeaways

1.

More inclusive to the full spectrum of ages and careers

2.

Illustrate the expanse of Techweek throughout a city

3.

Create a sense of excitement for upcoming cities

4.

Portray the significance of Techweek visiting smaller cities

5.

Develop a new editorial section

6.

Keep the logo, but refresh the brand colors

PROBLEM DISCOVERY

We conducted usability tests on the current website in order to better understand what could be improved upon through our designs.

Key Takeaways

1.

The process of buying a ticket and selecting a city was too difficult

2.

Overall the website was too text-heavy

3.

Users were confused about which events were associated with Techweek and each event’s location

4.

Pages lack visual direction and do not provide the user with a visual representation of their progress

5.

The website could not be identified as a tech-related conference

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

The next step was to consider who else was in this area of the industry, and how to visually differentiate Techweek from the rest of the pack.

Key Takeaways

1.

Brighter colors with a clean, minimal look

2.

Slight hints to technology in an abstract manner

3.

Create a sense of motion throughout the site with microinteractions, directed steps, and one main scrollable page

4.

Large pictures that show the attendees doing more than sitting in an auditorium

5.

Content overload is going to be an issue with any large conference or festival website

SYNTHESIZE

DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Express the Ecosystem

Portray Techweek as a festival that integrates with your city and local tech resources

Clear and Concise

Minimize text and organize information into digestible portions

Adaptability

Create a modular system to allow Techweek to make modifications as the company grows and enters new cities

Youthful and Inclusive

Express that the atmosphere is upbeat, and about tech, but inclusive for even those tangentially related to tech

Create a Sense of Space

Ensure the user knows where they are, and how to progress to the next step

SITE ARCHITECTURE

Through usability testing, I found that users lost their sense of progress within the large amount of pages. I remedied this using Axure to create a new site architecture and streamlined the process of navigating through the website.

EXPLORE

STYLE TILES

The first style tile, “Friendly Hipster”, centered around a techy atmosphere, but was still inclusive of all participants. Using Airbnb and InVision as inspiration, I paired round shapes with warm colors and a clean aesthetic.

The second style, “Pixel Fest”, references the tech world through subtle hints of code and pixels within a minimalist, but bold visual style. I also utilized elements from the Techweek logo to create a flag that symbolizes “taking over” a city.

SKETCHES/WIREFRAMES

I selected the "Pixel Fest" style because it showed properly showed the excitement and energy that Techweek was looking for. I proceeded to sketch out the website’s appearance and key functionalities in wireframes.

SIDEWALK

Sidewalk is a start-up that uses big data to track user interest and awards a score for the place’s influence in the area akin to the Klout Scoring. Originally, Sidewalk was heavily used in the tech industry, but they saw a chance for innovation in the liquor industry. We were brought on to help facilitate their move and redesign their dashboard to fit the new consumer industry.

Role

UX Designer

Deliverables

User & SME Interviews / Personas
Scenarios / Wireframes / Prototypes

Client

Sidewalk

CURRENT PRODUCT

HEURISITIC EVALUATION

Since Sidewalk already had a rough dashboard, we conducted a heuristic evaluation to determine some basic functionality problems.

DOMAIN RESEARCH

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

We completed a competitive analysis on data tools used in the liquor industry. As Sidewalk had predicted, there were many products tracking the market, but only a few leveraging big data. Furthermore, most of the services focused on the liquor industry were B2B rather than B2C.

INTERVIEWS

As there were not many companies using big data, it was important to validate the Sidewalk’s concept as something needed by the industry and reformat if necessary. We interviewed SME’s and potential users from the different positions within the retailer sphere.

First was a Chicago agency that acts as an intermediary between brands and retailers, then we talked to brand strategists for both Lagunitas and Bacardi. We asked them a series of basic domain questions to get a foundational understanding of the industry, their current process, and their pain points.

1.

What is your current process for finding opportunities for sales to on premise?

2.

What would you like to know about an on premise location place before you sell to them?

3.

What are your frustrations around keeping up to date with the data you use?

4.

What is your current process for choosing where to launch a new product?

Then, I ran a 5-second tests so that we could assess their mental models. Afterwards, I walked them through a concurrent think aloud on the rough dashboard to set expectations and their general ideas on the product.

Key Takeaways

1.

Many already had large amounts of data, but no way to make it actionable

2.

Still relied heavily on relationships to create business

3.

Enjoyed seeing which places were on the rise, but looking for more specifics as to why they were doing so well (e.g. the type of liquor and which social media outlet)

4.

Confused about the algorithm used to create an influence score

5.

Need to be able to see all the information on the same page, so they can do comparisons

6.

Needs to have a spreadsheet to see data, but looking for visual ways to also represent the data

7.

Looking for a reference to the age of the data

SYNTHESIZE

Affinity Diagramming

Taking the research into account, we began to sort the information looking for insights within the data. We then took those takeaways into actionable solutions through the creation of design principles.

Key Takeaways

1.

The relationship will always be a factor in the liquor industry

2.

Need to be able to make the system their own using personal data and taxonomy

3.

Looking for the most current information, because areas change often)

4.

Want to be able to see the data by area, and by individual business

5.

Actionable means that they can use it to convince their higher-ups that the decision is valid

6.

Desire the ability to break down by the type of bar, best selling liquor, and most popular social media

DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Granular & Macro Views

Give the user the ability to see social influence not only on a macro level, but on a minute level as well. Showing scale can help the user pinpoint strengths and weaknesses faster.

Layering

Users get overwhelmed if they see too much at once, showing only what needs to be seen can break up mental load. Providing relevant information on each screen helps the user to see points of opportunity.

Informed in Real Time

Information is king, but if you're not up to date, you’re behind. By providing a logical flow of new data and old data the user can more properly glen insights and expand their opportunities.

On-Demand

Ability to access the data when you need it, where you need it. The users are never in one place for too long, being able to look at their data keeps the business strong and well-informed.

Your Sidewalk

Not only lead to the insights, but give the opportunity to take action. Users need to not only synthesize the data, but be able to show those insights to uninformed participants.

PERSONAS

The promotional side of the liquor brand took two different forms, the brand managers and the brand representatives.

IDEATE

Wireframe for Brand Representative

In exploring the needs of the Brand Representative, we found that she would need to a way to see the data on-the-go and I created a mobile version for her. Her main task was to track where she has been, which place she should go next, and prep her for creating better relationships with owners.

Wireframe for Brand Manager

The Brand Manager would be more often in the office, so his execution of the system would focus more on the desktop. His main task was being able to see the data, in a way that leads to better insights based on the data that he can take to the higher ups.

‹ ›

PROTOTYPE & TEST

PROBLEM DISCOVERY

We created a prototype and conducted usability tests with participants in 1871 to uncover major problems within the wireframes. Taking the synthesis and this information back to the stakeholders, we were able to focus in the scope of the project to a better minimum viable product.

Key Takeaways

1.

Influence Score - The influence score is the main point of the product, but also the most confusing, we needed a way to better explain it.

2.

Map vs List - Need a way to see the list and the map separately and at the same time.

3.

Taxonomy - Not only wanted to be able to use pre-generated tags, but create a more tagging system that conformed to their way of doing business.

4.

Target the Brand Manager - They are the ones that need the data the most and have the power to get the system implemented in their companies.

5.

Get Rid of the CRM Parts - Many of the brands already have a way to track their sales, integrated with that is something that will be more feasible down the line.

6.

Desktop vs Mobile - Thinking of the Brand Manager, it was better to focus on the more used device.

7.

Overview vs Granularity - When testing, many people got confused about how getting so granular would help the brands make larger decisions.
